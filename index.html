📋 Referenced Laws & What They Mean
UAE Federal Law
Article 17 — Law of Civil Transactions (Federal Law No. 5/1985)
This is the foundation of the entire will. Article 17 allows non-UAE nationals to have their estate governed by the laws of their home country rather than UAE law. It means a Lebanese Christian like Chadi can direct that his estate be distributed according to his own wishes — not under UAE default rules or Sharia law.
Dubai Local Law
Dubai Law No. 15 of 2017 — Non-Muslim Wills & Inheritance
This Dubai-specific law created a dedicated system for non-Muslims to register and execute wills in Dubai. It established the DIFC Wills Service Centre and allows non-Muslims to freely distribute their UAE assets as they choose. It takes precedence over any general legislation on the same topic. This law is why Chadi's will can legally exclude Sharia law entirely.
UAE Federal Law
Federal Law No. 28 of 2005 — Personal Status Law
This is the UAE's main family and inheritance law, which applies Sharia principles to estate matters by default. For Muslims, this governs how estates are divided. For non-Muslims with a registered will (like Chadi), this law is overridden by Dubai Law No. 15/2017 and Article 17 of the Civil Transactions Law.
UAE Federal Law
Federal Decree-Law No. 14 of 2021 — Amending Civil Transactions Law
This is the most recent amendment to the Civil Transactions Law. It strengthened the rights of non-Muslims to have their wills and estates handled according to their own national laws or their expressed wishes. It reinforces that non-Muslim expatriates do not need to follow Sharia inheritance rules.
Lebanese Law
Lebanese Civil Law — Applicable as Home Country Law
Referenced in Clause 3(C) of the will as a fallback. If a UAE court refuses to enforce any part of Chadi's will, Lebanese law — the law of his country of origin — will be applied to resolve the dispute. Under Lebanese civil law for Christians, estates can be distributed freely by will without religious inheritance formulas.
Sharia Law
Islamic Inheritance Law (Faraid)
Sharia inheritance law (Faraid) prescribes fixed shares to family members — spouse, children, parents — regardless of the deceased's wishes. For example, a son receives double a daughter's share; non-family members receive nothing. This will explicitly and legally excludes Faraid from applying to Chadi's estate.

This law created the legal framework for non-Muslims to register wills in Dubai and have them enforced exactly as written. It takes precedence over all other general inheritance legislation. Without this law, a non-Muslim's estate in Dubai could default to Sharia distribution rules.
20Does being Christian affect how my will is treated in the UAE?▼
Yes — positively. As a non-Muslim, you have the legal right to opt out of Sharia inheritance entirely and distribute your estate freely. Muslims do not have this option under UAE law. (Dubai Law No. 15/2017)
21What would happen to my estate if I did NOT have this will — how would Sharia law divide it?▼
Without a registered will, UAE courts would apply Sharia inheritance rules (Faraid) by default, even for non-Muslims. Under Faraid:
  • Your children would receive fixed shares based on gender (sons get double daughters)
  • Your parents would receive a share if alive
  • Your executor (Ms. Yulia) would receive nothing as she is not a blood relative
  • Distribution would be entirely outside your control
(Federal Law No. 28/2005 — Personal Status Law)
22Under Sharia law, who would get what percentage of my estate?▼
Under Faraid, the division depends on surviving family members. As a general example with two daughters and no sons:
  • Two daughters together would receive a maximum of 2/3 of the estate
  • Remaining 1/3 would go to other heirs (parents, siblings) based on who is alive
  • Non-relatives (like Ms. Yulia) receive nothing
This is precisely why this will exists — to override this and give 50% to each daughter directly, managed by Ms. Yulia until they turn 18.
23Can Sharia law still interfere even though my will explicitly excludes it?▼
No — not if the will is properly registered. A registered non-Muslim will under Dubai Law No. 15/2017 is legally binding and Sharia law cannot override it. The only risk would be if the will is unregistered, improperly executed, or successfully challenged in court. (Dubai Law No. 15/2017)
24What is the difference between what Sharia law dictates versus what my will directs?▼
  • Sharia: Fixed shares by gender and blood relation. Non-relatives get nothing. No flexibility.
  • This will: Equal 50/50 split to both daughters. Ms. Yulia manages as trustee. Full flexibility.
  • Key difference: The will gives you complete control. Sharia removes it entirely.

Validity & Court Challenges ▲
57Under what circumstances can a court reject part of my will?▼
  • The provision violates UAE public policy
  • The provision is illegal under UAE law
  • The will was improperly signed or witnessed
  • A creditor has a legal claim that takes priority
  • A provision harms a minor's best interests
58If one clause is found invalid, does the whole will fail?▼
No. Clause 3(D) — the Severability Clause — ensures that if one provision is ruled invalid, all remaining provisions continue to be fully enforceable. The invalid clause is removed; the rest stands.
59Can family members challenge this will?▼
Anyone can attempt to challenge a will in court. However, a properly registered non-Muslim will in Dubai is very difficult to overturn. Grounds for challenge are limited to fraud, forgery, mental incapacity at time of signing, or undue influence. (Dubai Law No. 15/2017)
60What makes a will legally valid in the UAE?▼
For a non-Muslim will in Dubai, it must be: in writing, signed by the testator, witnessed by two witnesses, and registered with the DIFC Wills Service Centre or Dubai Courts. (Dubai Law No. 15/2017)
61Does the will need to be registered anywhere in the UAE?▼
Yes — registration is strongly recommended and practically essential. A will registered with the DIFC Wills Service Centre or Dubai Courts is immediately enforceable and very difficult to challenge. An unregistered will can still be submitted to court but carries more risk of delay and challenge. (Dubai Law No. 15/2017)

What Happens When You Die — The Practical Process ▲
65What exactly happens the moment I pass away — what is the sequence of events?▼
  • Step 1: Death certificate is issued by UAE hospital or relevant authority
  • Step 2: Family or Executor notifies relevant authorities, banks, and the will registry
  • Step 3: Banks freeze all accounts automatically upon notification
  • Step 4: Executor applies to UAE court (Dubai Courts or DIFC) for a Grant of Probate
  • Step 5: Court reviews and validates the registered will
  • Step 6: Court issues an order authorizing the Executor to act
  • Step 7: Executor presents court order to banks and institutions to unfreeze and transfer assets
  • Step 8: Debts, funeral costs, and claims are paid
  • Step 9: Remaining estate is distributed to beneficiaries per the will
66How does my Executor find out that I have died?▼
There is no automatic legal notification to your Executor. It is your family's or next of kin's responsibility to inform the Executor. This is why it is critical that your Executor's contact details are known to your family, and that your family knows who your Executor is and where the will is stored.
67How do banks find out that I have died — who notifies them?▼
Banks do not automatically know. Either the family, the Executor, or the court notifies them. Once a bank is officially notified — typically with a death certificate — they are legally required to freeze all accounts of the deceased to protect the estate. (UAE Central Bank regulations; Federal Law No. 5/1985)
68Do banks automatically freeze my accounts when I die — and why?▼
Yes — once notified of a death, banks are legally required to freeze all accounts. This protects the estate from being accessed without legal authority and ensures assets are distributed properly through the legal process. No one — including family members — can withdraw funds once accounts are frozen.
69Can my family access my bank accounts immediately after my death?▼
No. Once accounts are frozen, access is not permitted until the court issues a formal order authorizing the Executor to act. This is why it is advisable to keep some accessible emergency funds outside of sole-name accounts — such as a joint account — to cover immediate expenses like funeral costs.
70With a registered will, what is the step-by-step process my Executor follows?▼
  • 1. Obtain the official death certificate
  • 2. Locate the registered will (DIFC or Dubai Courts registry)
  • 3. File a probate application with the relevant court
  • 4. Submit: will, death certificate, Executor's ID, asset list
  • 5. Court validates the will and issues a Grant of Probate
  • 6. Executor presents the Grant of Probate to each bank and institution
  • 7. Banks unfreeze accounts and transfer assets to estate account
  • 8. Executor pays debts, expenses, and distributes per the will
71Does my Executor go to court first, or directly to the bank?▼
Court first — always. The Executor must obtain a court-issued Grant of Probate before any bank will act. No bank in the UAE will release frozen funds without a valid court order, regardless of what the will says.
72Can my Executor go directly to the bank with the will to unfreeze accounts?▼
No. A will alone is not sufficient to unfreeze bank accounts. The Executor must first obtain a Grant of Probate from the Dubai Courts or DIFC Court. Only then can they present the court order to the bank. The will is the foundation — the court order is the key.
73How long does the entire process take from death to distribution?▼
For a registered non-Muslim will in Dubai, the probate process typically takes between 4 to 12 weeks depending on complexity, asset types, and whether any disputes arise. Simple estates with a registered DIFC will can be resolved faster. Complex estates with property, business interests, or disputes may take longer.
74What documents does my Executor need to present to the court and banks?▼
  • Original death certificate (attested)
  • Registered will or certified copy
  • Executor's passport and ID
  • List of known assets and liabilities
  • Property title deeds (if applicable)
  • Bank account details
  • Court-issued Grant of Probate (for banks)
75What happens to my assets during the period between death and the court order?▼
All assets are frozen and legally protected. No one can access, sell, or transfer them. The estate effectively enters a holding period until the court issues the Grant of Probate. Property cannot be sold, and accounts cannot be debited during this time.
76Can my family pay for my funeral if accounts are frozen?▼
Bank accounts will be frozen, so the family will likely need to cover funeral costs from personal funds initially. Once the Grant of Probate is issued, the Executor can reimburse these costs from the estate as a priority expense. Some UAE banks may release a limited amount for funeral expenses upon a formal request — this varies by bank policy.
77What if my Executor is abroad when I die — is there a time limit to act?▼
There is no strict legal deadline to file for probate in the UAE, but delays can complicate matters — especially with frozen accounts and ongoing property obligations. The Executor can appoint a legal representative in the UAE to begin the process while they travel. Acting promptly is strongly advised.
78What happens if I die outside the UAE?▼
Your UAE assets are still governed by this will. The process remains the same — the Executor applies to UAE courts for probate. However, the foreign death certificate must be officially translated into Arabic and attested (by the UAE Embassy in that country and the UAE Ministry of Foreign Affairs) before it is accepted by UAE courts and banks. This may add time to the process.
